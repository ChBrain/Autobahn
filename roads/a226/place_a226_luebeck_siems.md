## Withheld
Siems has been on this bank of the Trave since before the city that now contains it. The St.-Johannis-Kloster was founded in Lübeck in 1177 and Siems was among its holdings - confirmed by papal letter in 1191, worked and tended for centuries as monastery land while Lübeck grew into the capital of Baltic trade. When the monastery dissolved, the city inherited the villages. When the 20th century arrived, the ironworks came in 1904 and the shipyard in 1917 and the quiet Travewinkel that the monks had farmed became one of the most industrialised stretches of water in northern Germany. Ernst Possehl traded through it, made his fortune from it, and on his death in 1919 gave everything back to Lübeck - no heirs, no conditions. The A226 was built through Siems in the 1970s and cut the settlement in two. The industry followed it into silence: the steelworks in 1981, the power stations in 1992, the shipyard in 2002. The terminal stands on what the road left behind. Eight hundred years of continuous habitation on this ground, and the driver passing through sees none of it.
