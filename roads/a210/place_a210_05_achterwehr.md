## Withheld
The name means "behind the weir" — the weir that controlled the Eider here long before any canal existed. When the Nord-Ostsee-Kanal was dug 1887–95, it cut through the Eider's drainage basin and lowered the Flemhuder See by seven metres, threatening to drain the surrounding farmland. A ring canal was built to hold the water level. When the NOK was widened 1907–14, dredged material filled the Flemhuder See completely, cutting off the village's water connection. A replacement canal three kilometres long was built by 1913 — the Achterwehrer Schifffahrtskanal — with a lock to bridge the seven-metre drop to the NOK. It carried grain barges for decades. The lock was decommissioned in 2001. The Eider now flows through it into the NOK.
