## Load Bearing
Remove this piece and the postwar BRD has no federal road policy. The Autobahn network inherited from the Third Reich sits unextended, unregulated, without a governance framework. Every subsequent minister inherits either the network Seebohm built or the regulatory model he imposed. Both are still present in the system in 2025.

## Apparent
A national motorway network extending to 1,400 km by 1966. A set of laws restricting truck dimensions, axle loads, and operating licenses: the Seebohm'sche Gesetze, printed in the Bundesgesetzblatt and enforced by the Kraftfahrt-Bundesamt in Flensburg, founded 1951. Physical form: grey asphalt, blue signs, green motorway markers, concrete kilometre posts at 100-metre intervals.

## Yearbook

**1949:** Seebohm takes office. The BRD inherits approximately 2,100 km of Autobahn, much of it damaged or incomplete. No federal framework for road governance exists yet.

**1951:** Kraftfahrt-Bundesamt founded in Flensburg. First federal authority for vehicle registration and road safety.

**1953:** Second truck trailer prohibited. Seebohm begins regulating the trucking industry to protect Deutsche Bundesbahn freight revenue. Industry protests.

**1954:** Law passed requiring 50% of all bulk freight transport to be carried by rail. Trucking industry begins lobbying campaign that will last decades.

**1955:** Verkehrsfinanzgesetz and Strassenentlastungsgesetz passed. Trucking costs increased by law to shift competitive balance toward rail.

**1956:** Sunday truck traffic prohibited. Maximum truck dimensions tightened: 24-tonne GVW, 8-tonne axle load, 14-metre length. German truck manufacturers forced to redesign their entire product lines. The unintended consequence: German trucks become the most efficient in Europe.

**1957:** Luftfahrt-Bundesamt founded. Federal aviation authority established.

**1960:** EWG harmonization pressure forces partial easing of truck restrictions. Rail and road now coexist under a revised framework. The era of pure rail protection ends.

**1960-1966:** Motorway construction accelerates. By 1966, over 1,400 km built under Seebohm's tenure. More motorway per capita than any other European nation. Federal road spending exceeds that of any comparable country.

**1966:** Seebohm leaves office after 17 years. The motorway network and its regulatory framework are intact. Georg Leber inherits both.

**1971:** Leber-Plan attempts to reverse Seebohm's road priority. Fails in the Bundestag. The Seebohm framework survives.

**1989-onwards:** Reunification adds the former DDR motorway network, much of it in poor condition. The 17 Verkehrsprojekte Deutsche Einheit extend the framework eastward.

**2025:** The Federal Transport Infrastructure Plan 2030 identifies the maintenance backlog of the network Seebohm built as one of the defining infrastructure challenges of the era.
